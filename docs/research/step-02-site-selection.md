# Step 2 Site Selection: Memphis Anchor

## Decision

Use a paired anchor for the MVP:

- Primary temple anchor: the early Ptah / Hwt-ka-Ptah cult precinct at Memphis.
- Primary living-city anchor: Kom el-Fakhry / Mit Rahina settlement evidence.

This gives the experience a strong sacred climax while keeping the residential street, craft activity, and Nile-side urban life tied to the best current evidence for the city itself. The tour should not claim to be a precise reconstruction of an excavated Old Kingdom Ptah temple plan. It should present a historically plausible early Memphis district whose sacred endpoint is an early Ptah precinct, with evidence labels separating confirmed, inferred, and speculative elements.

## Why This Anchor

The Temple of Ptah is the strongest temple choice for Memphis because institutional sources identify it as the main or most important Memphis temple. UNESCO describes the Temple of Ptah at Mit Rahina as the most important temple among the archaeological remains of Memphis, and also notes associated craftsmen's workshops, dockyards, arsenals, and residential neighborhoods around the temples and palaces. The British Museum identifies Hwt-ka-Ptah as the Great Temple of Memphis and locates it in the center of the Memphite ruin field near Mit Rahina.

Kom el-Fakhry is essential because it is the best route into the actual living city. The Memphis Kom el-Fakhry Archaeological Project describes Memphis as highly fragmented and says Kom el-Fakhry contains the earliest in-situ remains found at Memphis to date: a First Intermediate Period cemetery and a Middle Kingdom town. Recent ARCE event notes describe MKAP work uncovering houses, streets, everyday objects, and some of the earliest in-situ Old Kingdom remains at Memphis.

The Nile approach is plausible but must be handled carefully. Geoarchaeological work on the Saqqara-Memphis floodplain reports shifting Nile channels and states that the first capital of unified Egypt was established on the bank of a western Nile branch. That supports a river-edge arrival mood, but not an exact dock location.

## Candidate Comparison

| Candidate | Strengths | Weaknesses | MVP Fit |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Early Ptah / Hwt-ka-Ptah precinct | Strongest Memphis temple identity; central to city; well attested as the main Memphis temple; connected to craftsmen and creation theology. | Early Dynastic / Old Kingdom architecture is not securely known; visible remains are mostly later or fragmentary. | Best temple anchor, but exact layout must be inferred/speculative. |
| Kom el-Fakhry district | Best current evidence for ancient Memphis as a lived city; houses, streets, everyday objects, and early/Old Kingdom investigation. | Not a temple anchor; much direct architecture is Middle Kingdom or later. | Best residential/crafts anchor. |
| Memphis Hathor Temple | In-situ walls, columns, pylon, forecourt, pronaos, and ongoing documentation. | New Kingdom/Ramesside, not Early Dynastic or Old Kingdom. | Useful later-period reference, not the first MVP anchor. |
| Temple of Apis / Serapeum network | Rich cult story and strong later textual tradition. | Main Memphis Apis temple location is uncertain; not ideal for early Memphis street-walk MVP. | Later expansion candidate. |
| Saqqara Djoser complex / Heb-Sed court | Extremely strong Old Kingdom-adjacent architecture and reconstruction references. | Necropolis/royal funerary complex, not Nile-side living city district. | Visual/architectural comparator, not the main route. |
| Abu Ghurab / Abusir sun temples | Old Kingdom and more reconstructable than early Memphis temples. | Outside the compact Memphis city walk and not the White Walls identity. | Expansion/reference candidate. |

## Recommended MVP Route

1. Nile-side arrival, marked `inferred`.
   The visitor arrives from a Nile branch or canal edge. Boats, reeds, workers, and store activity are plausible because Memphis depended on river access, but the exact landing should remain inferred.

2. White-plastered edge / administrative threshold, marked `inferred`.
   Use whitewashed mudbrick or plastered wall language. The White Walls identity is attested in written tradition and possibly linked to Kom Tuman wall evidence, but the exact Old Kingdom wall remains debated.

3. Mudbrick residential street, marked `inferred`.
   Use Kom el-Fakhry and Memphite settlement archaeology as the main reference layer. Keep house placement and street plan provisional until specific plans and publications are licensed/reviewed.

4. Craftsmen area, marked `inferred`.
   Ptah's Memphis identity as creator/craftsman deity plus UNESCO's note of workshops, dockyards, and arsenals supports a craft district. Specific workshop layout is artistic reconstruction.

5. Early Ptah precinct exterior, marked `inferred`.
   The cult/temple identity is confirmed, but the Early Dynastic / Old Kingdom exterior form is not. The best first version is a walled precinct, simple sacred court, shrine massing, and craft/ritual approach rather than a later monumental pylon complex.

6. Painted shrine/interior, marked `speculative`.
   This is the emotional/cinematic climax, but the exact iconography, color program, and spatial plan must be treated as speculative until Step 3 source work gives us specific allowable references.

## Evidence Tags For The Current Prototype

- Confirmed: Memphis as an important ancient capital; Ptah/Hwt-ka-Ptah as the main Memphis temple; Kom el-Fakhry as a key settlement mound; the existence of Memphis temple/city archaeology around Mit Rahina.
- Inferred: Nile-side arrival, compact route continuity from river to street to precinct, mudbrick/plaster urban street, craft activity near the sacred/administrative district, early Ptah precinct exterior.
- Speculative: exact Old Kingdom temple plan, painted shrine layout, specific NPC routines, exact wall height, exact iconographic program, exact dock and street positions.

## Sources Checked

- [UNESCO: Memphis and its Necropolis](https://whc.unesco.org/en/list/86/) identifies the Temple of Ptah in Mit Rahina as the most important temple among Memphis remains and notes surrounding workshops, dockyards, arsenals, and residential neighborhoods.
- [British Museum: Temple of Ptah (Memphis)](https://www.britishmuseum.org/collection/term/x30320) identifies Hwt-ka-Ptah / Hut-Ka-Ptah as the Great Temple of Memphis and locates it in the center of the Memphite ruin field.
- [Egypt Ministry of Tourism and Antiquities: Temple of Ptah blocks](https://egymonuments.gov.eg/news/excavations-unveil-a-portion-of-the-temple-of-ptah/) reports inscribed blocks believed to belong to the Great Temple of Ptah in Memphis.
- [ARCE: Rediscovering the Lost City of Memphis](https://arce.org/event/arce-ne-beyond-the-pyramids-rediscovering-the-lost-city-of-memphis-by-dr-raghda-el-behaedi/) describes recent Kom el-Fakhry work on houses, streets, everyday objects, and early Old Kingdom remains.
- [Sphinx Foundation for Archaeological Research: Memphis Kom el-Fakhry Archaeological Project](https://www.sphinxarchaeology.org/komelfakhry) summarizes MKAP, the site's fragmented urban context, and the earliest in-situ remains known at Memphis.
- [ScienceDirect: Holocene alluvial history of the Saqqara-Memphis floodplain](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S027737911730269X) gives the Nile/floodplain context and shifting river-channel evidence.
- [ARCE: The White Walls of Memphis](https://arce.org/event/galina-belova-white-walls-memphis/) summarizes a proposed Kom Tuman wall identification and the white-plaster interpretation.
- [Penn Museum Bulletin: A First Season of Excavating in Memphis](https://www.penn.museum/sites/bulletin/4066/) explains excavation difficulty, groundwater limits, and the sparse early remains at Memphis.
- [ARCE: Memphis Hathor Temple](https://arce.org/event/chicago-the-memphis-hathor-temple-recent-work-and-new-discoveries/) records why the Hathor temple is better as a later-period comparator than the first Old Kingdom MVP anchor.
